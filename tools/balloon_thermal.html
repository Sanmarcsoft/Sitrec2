Physics & Math

1. Coordinate System & Geometry

Camera views balloon from distance d_cam at look-down angle θ. Constraint: bgDist = camDist + ballAlt / sin(θ).

2. Surface Normal & Reflection

Normal: N = -d_view·w + cam_right·u + cam_up·v. Reflected ray R = I - 2(I·N)N. R_z > 0 = sky, R_z < 0 = ground.

3. Key Insights

  • Center = retroreflector: Center reflects sky, not ground.
  • Hottest point NOT at bottom: Shortest slant path is below center but above limb.
  • Atmospheric shortcut: Balloon’s reflected path can be shorter than direct camera→ground.

4. Sensor Simulation

The sensor renders at native resolution with the specified HFOV. Digital zoom crops the center 1/Z fraction and scales with nearest-neighbor interpolation, preserving pixelation. Sub-pixel objects (radius < 0.5 px) use area-weighted fill factor f = πr² to blend balloon and background temperatures: T_pixel = f·T_ball + (1-f)·T_bg. Fixed-seed PRNG noise is added to the temperature map before colormap application.

5. Ground Exposure Mode

Sets display range tightly around the apparent ground temperature T_bg ± 0.55·ΔT_ground. Ground pixels span the full black-to-white range. Temperatures outside this range (sky reflections, cold sky) saturate to the display limits. In black-hot mode, cold reflected-sky pixels clip to white, accurately simulating how real FLIR AGC (Automatic Gain Control) exposes for the dominant scene element.

6. HDR Bloom (Rerecord)

Simulates re-recording a FLIR display with a camera. Standard HDR bloom pipeline:

  1. HDR mapping: Display pixels are converted to linear light values in [0, 1]. Pixels above 90% brightness are smoothly ramped up to 300% (3.0), representing the thermal radiance that was clipped by the display’s limited dynamic range. This is physically motivated: the sensor’s thermal signal continues linearly beyond what the display can represent.
  2. Focus blur: Gaussian blur simulating camera defocus (adjustable σ).
  3. EV exposure: Logarithmic exposure adjustment: gain = 2EV. Positive EV brightens the image, pushing more pixels into the HDR saturation region.
  4. PSF convolution: Separable Gaussian convolution models the camera lens Point Spread Function. In HDR space, the 300% bright pixels spread energy outward via the weighted average. After convolution, any pixel still above 1.0 clips to white, creating an enlarged white core. The grey falloff halo appears where the Gaussian tail drops below 1.0. Dark regions are largely unaffected because the PSF is energy-conserving—it redistributes but does not create energy.
  5. Clipping: Final values are clamped to [0, 255]. The result: a bright object appears significantly larger than its true angular size, with a hard white center and thin grey border—matching the characteristic “glowing orb” appearance in re-recorded FLIR footage.
